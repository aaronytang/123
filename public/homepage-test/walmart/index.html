Cutting merchant busywork across 6 global sourcing markets

Walmart's sourcing operation ran on email threads, manual review, and back-and-forth navigation. I led design on three connected products — a marketplace, automated quote checking, and a messaging platform — that replaced that with one workflow.

Context

Where the work was leaking

Journey mapping across merchants and sourcing teams exposed three drains: a cumbersome product-adding process, heavy drop-off between product selection and buy-plan submission, and excessive email between merchants and sourcing.

Merlin Marketplace — the daily workflow, without the detours.

Merlin · Solution 1

Product adding, streamlined to the task

Continuous improvement driven by merchant feedback and behavior data — not feature shipping for its own sake.

Merlin · Solution 2

Half the navigation, all of the focus

Merchants bounced between pages and layers to finish one task. Collapsing four navigation destinations to two kept them on the task instead of on the map.

Merlin · Solution 3

Conversations attached to products, not buried in inboxes

Integrating Flip messaging directly into the catalog lets any product spark a conversation that syncs to mobile — replacing the email chains that were the third major drain.

QC-x — quote checking that scales by ruleset, not headcount.

QC-x

Manual bulk review became a digital ruleset

Key-information checks that previously meant line-by-line human review now run against rulesets users define once. Scheduled, automated reporting followed — reviews that ran on people now run on rules.

Flip — the messaging platform, revamped for adoption.

Flip

Found the adoption blockers, then iterated fast on scarce engineering

A UX evaluation traced low feature adoption to information architecture, not missing features. With limited engineering, validation happened early and often before code — landing on a consistent experience across desktop and mobile.

Strategic trade-offs

What we chose — and what we deliberately didn't build

  • 01
    Three connected products over one monolith. Marketplace, checking, and messaging serve different jobs at different rhythms. Integration points (Flip-in-Merlin) delivered the ecosystem value without the monolith's complexity.
  • 02
    Template-first for QC-x, customization later. A rigid data template shipped automation a year sooner; per-department template editing was sequenced as a fast-follow once the core proved out.
  • 03
    Fixed Flip's architecture instead of adding features. The adoption problem was structural. New features on a confusing IA would have compounded the problem — so the IA got rebuilt first.

Post-launch

One connected workflow across 6 markets

Merchants add products, check quotes, and resolve questions inside one ecosystem — the email-and-spreadsheet layer this replaced is gone from the daily workflow across all six sourcing markets.
